# Classroom Signals Guide

**Purpose:** Shared **observational language** for teachers, teams, and administrators. Signals describe **what learning looks like** when it becomes visible—not labels on students.

Use with §7.3 of the [white paper](white-paper.md) and [Appendix E — E2](appendix-e.md#e2--what-counts-as-evidence-of-understanding).

---

## How to use this guide

1. **Observe** — note what the student did, said, or produced in context.
2. **Map** — connect observation to a **learning goal**, not a trait.
3. **Document** — use standardized phrasing below for report cards, rubrics, conferences.
4. **Calibrate** — compare exemplars with colleagues; adjust criteria, not standards.

---

## Standardized assessment phrases (§7.3)

Use when the evidence matches:

| Phrase | When it applies |
|--------|-----------------|
| *The student accurately represented the core concept through non-written expression.* | Performance, demonstration, oral explanation, visual model—pathway other than traditional essay |
| *The student demonstrated conceptual transfer by connecting ideas.* | Linked concepts across contexts, subjects, or examples (including in-role connections) |
| *The student articulated understanding after reflection, indicating durable sense-making.* | Post-engagement reflection shows settled understanding, revision of thinking, or integration over time |

## Signals by expressive pathway

### Performative / embodied

**Look for:**

- Actions align with the concept being assessed (not merely memorized script)
- Student can explain *why* they made choices when asked
- Adaptation when constraints appear (environment, materials, time)

**Do not document as achievement:**

- Costume or prop quality unrelated to content
- Compliance with format without conceptual accuracy

**Sample observation note:**  
*During the living museum, the student maintained accurate historical content while adapting the performance when a planned technical element was unavailable.*

---

### Verbal / oral

**Look for:**

- Accurate use of content vocabulary in explanation
- Logical sequence; responds to probing questions
- Coherence appropriate to age and task

**Do not document as achievement:**

- Fluency alone without accuracy
- Recitation without comprehension

---

### Reflective (delayed)

**Look for:**

- References **specific** work from the unit
- Shows change in thinking or integration ("I used to think… now I think…")
- Connects to learning goal, not generic effort

**Do not document as achievement:**

- Length or completion without content
- Generic statements applicable to any class

See [Appendix E — E3](appendix-e.md#e3--reflection-as-assessment-not-homework).

---

### In-role / narrative

**Look for:**

- Content knowledge accurate **within** the narrative frame
- Sustained context (character, perspective, constraints of task)
- Transfer between lesson content and in-role application

**Do not document as achievement:**

- Entertainment value without content
- Confusion of fiction with factual claims outside the task frame

---

## Red flags (not signals of learning)

- Polished product with **no** observable thinking process when process matters
- **Trait language** ("lazy," "distracted," "unmotivated") substituted for evidence
- **Completion** graded as understanding
- **AI-generated text** with no anchoring in student engagement (see E3 sequence)

---

## Quick calibration prompts (team use)

1. Would two teachers agree this observation proves the **same learning goal** as a written artifact?
2. Is our language describing **evidence** or **character**?
3. If we removed this pathway, would we **lose accuracy** for any students we already know understand the content?
