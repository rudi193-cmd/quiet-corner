# Appendix A — Observational Case Studies

**Context:** Formal classroom observation during a school-wide **living history museum** event. Students portrayed historical figures using prepared research, in-character interaction, and optional props or performance elements. Pseudonyms are used throughout. Grades are approximate at time of observation.

**Ethical note:** These cases describe **observable demonstration of content knowledge**, not student traits, diagnoses, or labels. The pedagogical point is assessment **visibility**, not characterization of individual learners.

---

## Case 1 — Beethoven (Grade 4)

### Setting

A fourth-grade student participated in the living museum as a historical figure associated with music. The student had prepared a performance that included both vocal and instrumental elements.

### Observation

The student arrived with a personal keyboard for the performance segment. Power was unavailable at the assigned station—a common constraint in improvised event layouts, not a student error.

Rather than reducing the demonstration to the minimum viable format (vocal only, or omission of the instrumental component), the student:

- Sang the main thematic material accurately
- Simultaneously **mimed keyboard performance**, maintaining the embodied representation of the instrumental part that could not be produced acoustically

The performance integrated **two expressive channels** (vocal + embodied) under constraint. The content—musical and historical knowledge tied to the portrayed figure—was visible in a form a written checkpoint would not have requested or captured.

### Assessment visibility note

Narrow written or timed formats would likely have **under-represented** the student's command of the material. The museum pathway made understanding **legible** through performance.

### Pathways demonstrated

- Performative / embodied demonstration
- Verbal / musical expression without reliance on a written artifact

## Case 2 — Mozart (Grade 5)

### Setting

A fifth-grade student participated in the same living museum event, portraying a historical figure with a well-documented biography including specific dates and facts.

### Observation

During typical classroom seat work, this student often appeared highly active and easily redirected by environmental stimuli—common at this developmental stage and not, in itself, evidence about content knowledge.

During the museum performance, the student:

- **Maintained character** throughout the interaction window
- Recalled factual content **without reference** to prepared notes visible at the station
- Independently connected a calendar discussion from the immediate pre-event context to the portrayed figure's birth date—demonstrating **transfer** between a recent classroom reference and in-character knowledge

An observer relying only on conventional seat-work signals might form an inaccurate picture of the student's focus and retention. The museum pathway surfaced **focus, recall, and connection** aligned to the learning goal.

### Assessment visibility note

This is a **false-negative risk** case: performance in one channel (seat work) poorly predicted demonstration in another (in-role, public explanation). Expanded evidence pathways increase **accuracy**, not leniency.

### Pathways demonstrated

- Persona-based / narrative framing of knowledge
- Verbal explanation without written artifact
- Conceptual transfer (connecting ideas across contexts)

---

## Cross-case synthesis

| Dimension | Beethoven (G4) | Mozart (G5) |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Event** | Living museum | Living museum |
| **Constraint or context shift** | Technical affordance unavailable; adapted performance | In-role vs. typical seat-work setting |
| **What became visible** | Integrated musical/embodied demonstration | Sustained recall, character maintenance, transfer |
| **Risk without pathway** | Under-capture of integrated understanding | Misread of capability from narrow daily signals |

Both cases support the paper's central claim: **when only narrow evidence counts, capable learners are under-seen.** Multiple expressive pathways refine what counts as evidence without changing the underlying standard—*what the student knows and can demonstrate about the content*.
